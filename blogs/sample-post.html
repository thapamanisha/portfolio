Finding silence in Langtang.

December 2, 2025 7 min read By Manisha Thapa

There is a particular kind of silence in the high Himalayas that feels almost consensual — as though the mountains and the wind and the occasional dzo bell have all agreed that nothing more needs to be said. I went to Langtang in late autumn, after a year that had felt, in my own language, far too loud.

I had been running a menstrual-health research study, facilitating youth gatherings across three provinces, and trying to finish a grant application in between. The body keeps the score, people say; mine had started to keep a very detailed, very impatient one. So I packed a small pack, left my laptop behind, and walked.

The trail does not care what you've accomplished this year. The trail only cares whether you can carry your own water and sleep through the cold.

On walking alone

Solo female travel in Nepal is a politically charged phrase. It carries other people's anxieties more than your own. In Kathmandu, a relative will warn you about "the boys in the hills"; in the hills, a tea-house owner will warn you about "the boys in the city". Everywhere, the threat is relocated to somewhere just over the ridge. I've learned to listen for the real warnings — about weather, about altitude, about water — and to let the other ones wash off.

What walking alone gave me, on this trip, was an unfamiliar experience: no one to perform for. Not even kindly. Nobody to narrate the view to, nobody to prove a point to, nobody to reassure. Just me and a rhythm of four steps, one breath.

Altitude & attention

Above 3,500 metres, your attention narrows whether you like it or not. The things that felt important at the trailhead — emails, the state of a paper draft, a small political feud in a WhatsApp group — just stop mattering. You notice water, breath, warm socks, and the way clouds rearrange themselves around the valley in the late afternoon.

I started paying attention to the women on the trail. The didi at the bhatti in Lama Hotel who ran a tea-house, a garden, and three children with calm precision. The Tamang woman spinning wool in a doorway at Mundu. The two German women in their sixties who passed me on the descent, not even slightly winded. The quiet lesson was that competence, at altitude, doesn't announce itself.

Coming down

The descent is its own kind of research. You walk out with material — visual, emotional, intellectual — that you have to sit with for months before you know what any of it meant. I wrote very little in my notebook. What I did write, in black ink on the last morning in Kyanjin, was this: the work I want to do is the work that lets me go to places like this without feeling like I'm hiding from it.

That sentence has stayed with me since. It's begun to shape my MPH applications, my day-to-day decisions at UFC, and, quietly, the kind of art I'm making. I don't think advocacy has to be loud to be serious. The mountains seem to agree.
